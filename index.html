Henry K. Young

is 22 years old from Portland, Oregon. He is a student at Whitman College majoring in Theory and Practice in Digital Design. The individually planned major combines new media art, computer science, and art history with a focus on modern art and architecture. He realized he was interested in design in highschool after taking a summer course at Sci-Arc. Henry uses processing, figma, and various coding languages to create interactive interfaces, reflecting his Brutalist inspired style. He is familiar with python, C++, java, and html/javascript. Henry hopes to explore a career in User Experience Design or Architecture.

Another World

Our goal for the project was to see how we can transport the user into a different world. We accomplished this by changing the environment that surrounded them with a single event in A-Frame. Henry proposed that we create the different environment through an AI generator called “ASCII” (https://ascii-generator.site), which transforms the original library image into monochrome pixels. We then overlapped this new layer onto our original image. During the coding process, we applied an event where using the user's cursor would make the 1st image disappear. By doing this, it would enable a new view for the user, as if they’ve been transported into a unique dimension. Mateus used the animation event on A-Frame, where the user’s cursor location initiates a transition between one image from the other by visibility. The demo video included in the post explains our process in doing so.

Design Process:

We initially planned to develop the project outside A-Frame because of its limitations. However, while the process was ongoing, we learned that our HTML skills couldn’t suffice the initial plans. We thought about changing the camera data of the user so that they could experience multidimensional travel, but that was hard to figure out. Therefore, because of A-Frame's basic tools, we decided to commit to the project using that framework. While looking through A-Frames “entities”, I noticed that the skybox can be manipulated by the user, which was crucial for the change of environment. However, the question was “how” the skybox could be interacted with. The approach to this was using a mouse click function. But, after an hour of finding a mouse click function for A-Frame, we couldn’t find any information on that. The alternate plan was to use the location of the mouse instead of clicking. Therefore, to initiate the change of the skybox, the mouse had to be focused on a button. We didn’t have opportunity to get feedback before the publication of the project. But, with our best judgement, we liked the function of the concept. It was quite the experience overall!

Design for Dimensions


In my design for dimensions project, I focused on the large multi touch display. I chose these dimensions of a screen because it seemed like a wonderful challenge designing for a screen much larger and much less common than what is widely used at the moment. The format meant that you would be able to fit a lot of information onto one screen, but it meant that you would have to be wise about how you presented that information so as not to overwhelm the user. Using the example of the Microsoft surface hub, I saw that the screen is split into four parts, which was a useful limitation. As shown in my mood board below, I used TV and multi tasking layouts for inspiration as they organize large lists and a lot of information in a way that is still understandable to the user. The end result is an interface with four simultaneous pages that can be mixed and matched freely, while still being resemblant of a typical website.

Mood Board As interesting as having text and images that would span a massive screen would be, in this case it would be impractical and would negatively impact the viewing experience. Consequently I chose to use the guidelines provided and divide my layout into four parts as well, giving us four smartphone-like ratio screens to work with. I decided to make a main page, a page for faculty, a page for courses, and a page for the facilities. While the dense pages with large paragraphs are still necessary for the website, I chose pages with images and lists as they lend themselves better to demonstrating the multi touch scrolling interface I was designing. As for the aesthetics of the project, as a group we decided to follow the example of the Whitman website fairly closely, focussing more on the functionality and the compatibility with the screens than with the look of the website. Many assets of the design process could be shared and reused between members of the group while others had to be completely reworked in order to fit the screen.

In terms of the overall layout, many of the decisions had to do with the usage of space and the necessary information. The screen is large but meant to be viewed from a distance, so text and images need to be the correct size. For many of the pages the concept was to have primary information ready to be viewed from a distance and the secondary information with much more detail to be available in pop up boxes if needed. I experimented with several different ways of presenting the information, as can be seen in my sketches below, and ultimately concluded that in most cases it makes sense to contain the modules/pieces of data within boxes in order to give the user a prompt/sign that it can be tapped for more information.

I had several ideas that I wanted to incorporate that I wasn’t able to implement within the relatively short timeline we were given. If I could build on the project, I would make the prototype have alternating tabs so that any tab of the design could be placed anywhere on the screen. This would take either a considerable amount of time or a professional level of Figma experience. I would have also liked to make variable list components that would expand when tapped and resize the items in the list accordingly. This would also be very ambitious, as this takes a deep knowledge of figma components and auto layout that I am less familiar with. The more complicated interactions we were able to implement were dialogue/pop up boxes with more information, as well as scrollable lists. This makes the prototype far more realistic and immersive when demonstrating our design.

Overall, I wanted the website to still look and feel familiar while making use of this new format. Compared to the other screen dimensions, this allowed for a lot of flexibility in terms of how to display the information. However, when given an exciting new format like this, it is important to keep the information digestible and not overdo the possibilities of a larger screen. The website is similar to the current Whitman website in that it is professional and welcoming, but this new site allows for a better hierarchy/system of organization and it makes far more information accessible at any given time.

Designing with Data


For this project, I decided to look the correlation between knowledge of the past and optimism about the future. After learning about Nirvana in one of my classes, I became really interested in generational attitudes, especially regarding optimism and apathy. The first study included only the percentage of each category of knowledge that thought the world would be better off. To get a more accurate picture of the data, I wanted to see what proportion of people fit into which category, so I did some research and found the original study. The data is presented above.

For my sketch, I wrote a program in processing that draws a grid representing the data. Originally, I was planning on trying to represent the data with brains or some other representation of knowledge, however this proved to be too complicated in processing. I did, however, use the same color scale they used to represent no knowledge through very good knowledge, red through green respectively. I made it so that the data would determine the drawing, meaning you could adjust the proportions/numbers and the grid would change accordingly. I also made the grid respond to mouse position, so that when you move the mouse from left to right the sample size grows so that you can see small sample sizes and large ones. I was surprised to find that sample size affects my perception of the proportions, suggesting that we don’t see proportional data as objectively as we think. I also made it so that you could hover over the specific category of development knowledge and it would isolate that group so you could see the proportion of optimists to pessimists on their own. Originally, I wanted to make it so that you could only see the proportion of optimists to pessimists when you hovered over the category, but ultimately I decided that it was more beneficial to see the data as a whole so that you could derive the conclusion of the data more easily. While I don’t agree with the data necessarily, it suggests that people with more knowledge of world events and the past are more optimistic about the future of the world than those with less knowledge. I think this representation appeals to our senses and emotions because it encourages a certain level of playfulness and experimentation in the interaction. The ability to affect the way the data is presented also gives the user more flexibility and customization, allowing them to see the data in a way that is most understandable to them.
